Shock
13 QUESTIONS NEW CONTENT Dr. Mook · 2 hours Apply + Analyze dominant
Master Cheat Sheet — All Shock Types
TypeMechanismBPHRKey FindingVasopressor / Treatment
Hypovolemic ↓ intravascular volume → ↓ venous return → ↓ CO ↓ ↑ ↓ UO, peripheral hypoperfusion 2 large-bore IVs (14–16g), blood/IVF. Vasopressors if needed.
Cardiogenic ↓ CO from pump failure → ↓ tissue perfusion ↓ ↑ Crackles, BNP↑, cardiac enzymes↑ Dobutamine (inotropy), Dopamine, Norepinephrine. Monitor fluid overload.
Septic Exaggerated infection response → vasodilation, maldistribution ↓ ↑ Persistent hypotension despite 30 mL/kg IVF Norepinephrine (1st line), Epinephrine. ABX + cultures first.
Neurogenic SCI → loss of SNS tone → massive vasodilation ↓ ↓ BRADYCARDIA + hypotension = hallmark Phenylephrine, Dopamine, Atropine for bradycardia. Cautious fluids.
Anaphylactic Allergen → massive vasodilation + ↑ cap permeability ↓ ↑ Laryngeal edema, bronchospasm, angioedema Epinephrine IM FIRST, antihistamines, H2 blockers, corticosteroids.
Broad Pathophysiology
  • Normal: ATP made aerobically (efficient)
  • Shock state: cells lack blood → anaerobic metabolism → lactic acid buildup
  • Cell swelling → ↑ membrane permeability → electrolyte shifts → cell death
  • Body compensates: ↑ SNS → ↑ HR, ↑ BP, ↑ RR; RAAS → ↓ UO
Stages of Shock
  • Compensatory: Body maintains perfusion. Pallor, tachycardia, cool/clammy skin, tachypnea, confusion. Reversible if treated.
  • Progressive: Compensatory mechanisms fail. ↓ BP + CO, leaky vessels, crackles. Goal: prevent MODS.
  • Irreversible: Profound hypotension/hypoxemia, multi-organ failure, recovery unlikely.
Nursing Priorities — All Shock
  • ABCs first
  • Mental status assessments
  • Strict I&Os (hourly UO goal ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Body temp + skin changes
  • Tissue perfusion evidence: cap refill, pulses, LOC
  • Avoid organ complications from prolonged hypoperfusion
Cardiogenic — Pharmacology
  • Dobutamine — inotrope, ↑ contractility
  • Dopamine — chronotrope + inotrope, ↑ HR + contractility
  • Nitroglycerin — vasodilates coronary arteries
  • Norepinephrine / Epinephrine — vasopressors
  • Morphine — pain control
  • Labs: BNP↑, cardiac enzymes↑, lactate↑
  • SpO₂ goal: 95% (2–6L NC)
Swan-Ganz (PA Catheter): PAP 15–30/5–15 mmHg; PAWP 10–20 mmHg

Neurogenic = only shock with BRADYCARDIA

Transplantation
12 QUESTIONS NEW CONTENT Prof. Cleary · 2 hours
Types of Transplants
  • Xenotransplant — animal to human
  • Allotransplant — human to human (most common)
  • Autotransplant — self-donor (e.g., skin graft)

Organ Viability Windows

  • Heart/Lung: 4–6 hrs
  • Liver: 8–12 hrs
  • Pancreas: 12–18 hrs
  • Kidney: 24–36 hrs
UNOS Criteria
  • ABO blood type compatibility
  • HLA typing + CPRA score
  • Physical size match
  • Wait time + medical urgency + geography

Contraindications

  • Active cancer
  • Severe infection
  • Active substance use disorder
  • Severe comorbidities
  • Compliance concern with post-tx management

S/S of rejection: fever, fatigue, ↓ organ function, pain/swelling at site
Immunosuppressants
  • Tacrolimus (Prograf) — calcineurin inhibitor, backbone agent
  • Cyclosporine (Neoral) — alternative calcineurin inhibitor
  • Mycophenolate mofetil (CellCept) — antiproliferative
  • Thymoglobulin — induction agent (T-cell depletion)
  • Prednisone — corticosteroid
  • Ganciclovir — antiviral prophylaxis (CMV)
Clozapine → agranulocytosis. Tacrolimus → nephrotoxicity. Monitor levels!
Post-Transplant Nursing Priorities
  • Airway + hemodynamic monitoring
  • Organ perfusion assessment
  • Fluid + electrolyte balance
  • Pain control
  • INFECTION PREVENTION — #1 ongoing concern
  • Monitor for rejection (fever, ↓ function, site pain)
GVHD — Graft vs Host Disease (HSCT)
  • Occurs in 30–50% of allogenic HSCT recipients
  • Donor T-cells attack recipient's tissues
  • Acute: within 100 days → skin rash, diarrhea, hepatomegaly
  • Chronic: after 100 days
  • Treatment: immunosuppressants + corticosteroids
Engraftment = WBC, RBC, platelets begin to ↑ after HSCT
Key Patient Teaching
  • Take immunosuppressants as prescribed — never skip
  • Avoid crowds + wear mask (infection exposure)
  • Monitor for rejection signs
  • Regular follow-up appointments
  • Avoid contact sports
  • No fresh fruits/vegetables (neutropenic precautions)

Burns & Stevens-Johnson Syndrome
15 QUESTIONS — HIGHEST PRIORITY NEW CONTENT Dr. Bell (WB) · 2.5 hours
⚠️ Burns/SJS = 15 Qs (~18% of the exam) — the single highest-weighted topic. Master this section first. Primary causes of burn mortality: Sepsis and Fulminant MSOF.
Initial Assessment Priority — C-ABC
In burn/trauma: C-ABC (not ABC) — Control hemorrhage first, then Airway, Breathing, Circulation
  • Primary survey — address immediate life-threatening injuries (hemorrhage, pneumothorax/hemothorax)
  • Prevention of shock
  • Prevention of respiratory distress
  • Detection & treatment of concomitant injuries
  • Wound assessment & care
NCLEX trap: "What is the initial nursing priority for a burn patient?" → Answer = Airway/Circulation (C → evaluate airway and circulation), NOT pain, NOT Rule of 9s, NOT IV access.
3 Phases of Burn Care
  • 1. Initial/Emergent Phase — onset of injury through fluid resuscitation completion. Focus: shock prevention, respiratory distress, wound assessment.
  • 2. Acute/Intermediate Phase — initial diuresis through wound closure. Focus: respiratory/circulatory status, debridement, pain, thermoregulation, nutrition, infection prevention, early mobility.
  • 3. Rehabilitation Phase — wound closure through return to optimal function. Focus: scar/skin management, PT/OT, psych support, pain/itch management, nutrition, long-term follow-up.

TBSA Assessment — Rule of Nines
Gold standard for calculating TBSA% (Lund-Browder method is more precise for peds)
Body Region% TBSA

Anterior trunk18%
Posterior trunk18%

Perineum/genitalia1%
TOTAL100%

Burn Zones (Jackson's)

  • Zone of Coagulation — center, irreversible cell death
  • Zone of Stasis — middle, salvageable with adequate perfusion
  • Zone of Hyperemia — outer, inflammation, will recover
Fluid resuscitation saves the zone of stasis!
Parkland Formula — TBSA >20%
Thermal/Chemical Burns (2nd, 3rd, 4th°):
2 mL LR × TBSA% × kg = total over 24 hrs
Electrical Burns (2nd, 3rd, 4th°):
4 mL LR × TBSA% × kg = total over 24 hrs
  • ½ given over first 8 hours — from time of BURN (not arrival!)
  • Remaining ½ given over next 16 hours
  • Fluid = Lactated Ringer's (LR)
  • Urine output = standard indicator of fluid response
  • Electrical burns: goal UO = 75–100 mL/hr
  • Fluid is NOT titrated to vital signs — titrate to UO
  • Fluid administration is NOT held based on BP alone
Timing is the MOST important factor — begins at time of burn and covers the next 24 hours.
Example: 120 kg patient with 36% TBSA thermal burn → 2 × 36 × 120 = 8,640 mL total. Give 4,320 mL in first 8 hrs.
Inhalation Injury — Airway Priority

Suspect Inhalation Injury When:

  • Soot around mouth or nares
  • Singed nose hairs or facial hair
  • Facial or oropharyngeal edema (peaks at 24 hours)
  • Voice hoarseness
  • Audible wheezing that stops = DANGER (airway may be closing)
Wheezing that disappears → notify provider IMMEDIATELY. This is NOT improvement — it means the airway is occluding. Do NOT document as improvement or wait.

Interventions:

  • Early intubation before edema peaks
  • High-flow O₂ (100% via NRB)
  • Bronchodilators, nebulized treatments
  • Continuous SpO₂ monitoring (but pulse ox unreliable with CO poisoning)
Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning
  • Leading cause of death from fire + accidental poisoning
  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless — rapidly crosses lung membrane
  • CO has higher affinity for Hgb than O₂ → forms carboxyhemoglobin (COHb)
  • Pulse ox will be falsely NORMAL — use co-oximetry
  • COHb >10% = strong indicator of inhalation injury
  • COHb 61–80% = almost always fatal

S/S of CO Poisoning:

  • SOB, headache, confusion/AMS
  • Muscle weakness, dizziness, blurred vision
  • Red cherry lips/face OR pale skin
Treatment: 100% O₂ via NRB. Cyanide kit may also be needed (house fire exposure).
Electrical Burns
  • Assess: voltage, amperage, resistance, duration, pathway (entry/exit)
  • Internal damage often worse than external appearance
  • Anticipate: cardiac arrest, dysrhythmias, massive myoglobin release
  • Treatment: increase UO to 75–100 mL/hr (renal protection from rhabdomyolysis)
  • Use 4 mL LR × TBSA% × kg (not 2 mL)
  • Monitor CK, myoglobinuria (dark/red/pink urine)
  • Cardiac monitoring (telemetry)
Chemical Burns
  • Tissue damage continues until chemical is removed or inactivated
  • Dependent on: strength/concentration, quantity, duration of contact, mechanism
  • Decontamination (DECON): remove contaminated clothing, rinse ALL exposed areas with running water
  • Flush eyes if exposed
  • Do NOT neutralize acid with base (exothermic reaction = more damage)
Burn Shock — Pathophysiology (TBSA >20%)

Vascular

  • ↑ capillary permeability → capillary leak → fluid shift to tissues
  • Loss of plasma proteins & electrolytes
  • ↑ interstitial fluid = edema
  • ↓ intravascular volume = hypovolemia/burn shock

Systemic Response

  • Release of inflammatory mediators (cytokines)
  • Activation of sympathetic nervous system
  • ↑ catecholamines → hypermetabolic state
  • Suppressed anabolic hormones

Organ Effects

  • Pulmonary: upper airway obstruction, bronchospasm, ↑ COHb, PE, ARDS
  • Cardiac: ↓ CO, cardiac depression, flash pulmonary edema risk
  • Renal: AKI (hypovolemia), hematuria, myoglobinuria, ↑ bladder pressure
  • GI: paralytic ileus, stress ulcers (Curling's), impaired motility
  • Skin: impaired thermoregulation, altered immune defenses, insensible fluid loss
Nursing Management — Acute/Intermediate Phase
  • Airway + breathing — always first priority
  • Fluid resuscitation per Parkland (titrate to UO, not VS)
  • Pain management: long-acting analgesics, high-dose narcotics/sedatives (burns metabolize meds faster), avoid NSAIDs
  • Check substance use history — higher doses typically needed, no judgment
  • Alternative therapies: virtual reality for procedural pain
  • Hypothermia prevention (warm environment, warm fluids)
  • Nutrition: high-protein, high-calorie (hypermetabolic state)
  • Early mobility + positioning to prevent contractures
  • Weight during dressing changes (prior to application)
  • Always assess pulses — especially with circumferential dressings
  • Tetanus prophylaxis status — assess and update as needed
Wound Care & Debridement

4 Types of Debridement

  • Natural: spontaneous separation of devitalized tissue
  • Mechanical: surgical tools used to remove eschar
  • Chemical: topical enzymatic agents (Silvadene, heavy metals)
  • Surgical: surgical removal before natural debridement occurs

Grafting

  • Autograft — from patient's own body (donor site)
  • Allograft — from another human (living or cadaveric)
  • Xenograft — from animal (heterograft)
  • Keep bulky gauze over new grafts: clean & dry
  • Daily dressing changes (or more frequent PRN)
  • Skin grafts cause significant blood and heat loss — monitor closely
Escharotomy & Fasciotomy

Escharotomy

  • Surgical linear excision through eschar
  • Indicated for circumferential burns (central or peripheral)
  • Prevents compartment syndrome
  • Assess: pulses, bladder pressures, ventilator compliance

Fasciotomy

  • Excision through fascia — deeper than escharotomy
  • Releases pressure from deep compartments
  • Promotes muscle and nerve viability
  • Opens one or multiple muscle compartments
Infection Prevention — Burn Patient
  • Infection/sepsis = #1 cause of death in burn patients
  • Frequent hand washing (all staff)
  • Proper PPE when entering burn patient room
  • No fresh flowers, no fresh fruits/vegetables/outdoor foods
  • Daily wound and dressing changes (debridement)
  • Topical antimicrobials (Silvadene = silver sulfadiazine, antibiotic ointments)
  • Assess tetanus status, update as needed
  • Monitor for signs of sepsis continuously
📌 Stevens-Johnson Syndrome is a separate testable topic — Dr. Bell covered this in the same lecture. Know SJS vs TEN distinction, causative drugs, and that management mirrors burns.
Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) & TEN
SJS: <10% TBSA epidermal detachment
Overlap: 10–20% TBSA
TEN (Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis): >30% TBSA

What is it?

  • Rare genetic skin condition triggered by medications
  • Widespread erythema → epidermal detachment/sloughing → erosion formation
  • Mortality: 1–5% (SJS) — higher with TEN
  • 75% triggered by medication reaction

Causative Drugs (must know):

  • Sulfonamides (most common)
  • Anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin)
  • NSAIDs
  • Allopurinol
SJS — Risk Factors & Clinical Manifestations

Who is at risk?

  • Immunocompromised (HIV/AIDS, carcinomas)
  • Personal or family history of SJS
  • Current use of sulfonamides, NSAIDs, anticonvulsants
  • Genetic factors

Initial Manifestations (flu-like first):

  • Burning/itching, tenderness of skin
  • Fever, cough, sore throat, headache
  • Severe malaise, arthralgias
  • Then: rapid onset of erythema involving much of the skin surface and mucous membranes
  • Mucosal involvement: eyes, mouth, genitals
SJS Nursing Interventions & Treatment
#1 Priority: STOP the causative medication IMMEDIATELY
  • Maintain skin & mucous membrane integrity
  • Ensure appropriate fluid/volume status
  • Prevent hypothermia
  • Monitor and manage complications
  • Relieve pain
  • Reduce anxiety
  • Treat like a burn patient (same supportive care principles)
  • ICU-level management for severe cases
SJS/TEN = burns equivalent in nursing management. Identify and stop the drug first, then treat supportively.
Key NCLEX Pearls — Burns & SJS
Initial priority for any burn patient = Airway and Circulation (C-ABC), NOT pain, NOT Rule of 9s first
Wheezing that disappears → call provider IMMEDIATELY. Not an improvement.
Pulse ox is UNRELIABLE with CO poisoning — use co-oximetry
Parkland timing starts at time of BURN, not ED arrival
Thermal/Chemical burns = 2 mL LR × TBSA% × kg; Electrical = 4 mL LR
Full thickness burns = painless (nerves destroyed); Partial thickness = most painful
Electrical burns: urine output goal = 75–100 mL/hr (rhabdomyolysis/renal protection)
SJS = stop the causative drug first. Most common triggers: sulfonamides, anticonvulsants, NSAIDs, allopurinol
Burn patients metabolize pain meds faster → require higher doses; avoid NSAIDs
Older adults have greater mortality with similar burn severity due to comorbidities and thinner skin
Sepsis, SIRS & DIC
SEPSIS: 3Q · DIC: 3Q = 6 TOTAL Dr. Mook · Cumulative
Sepsis Criteria + 1-Hour Bundle

Criteria (2 of these + suspected/confirmed infection)

  • Fever OR hypothermia
  • HR >90
  • RR >22
  • SBP <100
  • Altered mental status
  • Oliguria, ↑ lactate, ↑ or ↓ WBC

1-Hour Bundle (CALFV)

  • C — Cultures x2 (aerobic + anaerobic) BEFORE ABX
  • A — ABX (broad spectrum, large bore IV)
  • L — Lactate level (remeasure if initial >2)
  • F — Fluids: 30 mL/kg crystalloid (LR)
  • V — Vasopressors if MAP <65 despite fluids (via central line, ICU)
MAP >65 = goal for sepsis patient. Lactate normal: 0.5–2.2

DIC — Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation
Both bleeding AND clotting simultaneously — always a sign of underlying condition
  • Mechanism: excessive clotting → fibrinolytic system triggered → anticoagulants released → BLEEDING
  • Lots of tiny clots in microcirculation → organ failure

Labs

  • Platelets ↓, Fibrinogen ↓
  • PT ↑, PTT ↑, D-dimer ↑
  • Everything else ↑

Management

  • Treat underlying cause first
  • FFP (addresses coags fast)
  • Platelets when <20,000
  • Cryo replaces fibrinogen + factors 5 & 7
  • Heparin is last resort
Nursing: HOB 30°, stool softeners, avoid IM injections, rectal probes, clopidogrel/aspirin
Blood Transfusion Safety
  • 2 licensed personnel verify + 2 patient identifiers
  • Assess VS before, after 15 min, and at completion
  • Start slow, stay at bedside for first 15 min
  • Discontinue blood after 4 hours of hanging
  • Warm blood if patient is hypothermic
  • Have calcium available (hypocalcemia risk with massive transfusion)
  • Mass transfusion ratio: 1:1:1 (pRBCs : FFP : Platelets)

Upper GI Bleed
  • Causes: ulcers, varices, H. pylori, NSAID use, liver disease
  • S/S: black tarry stools (melena), coffee-ground emesis, hematemesis, hypovolemic shock

ARDS & Pneumothorax
4 QUESTIONS Dr. Jayasinghe · Cumulative
ARDS Criteria (All 5 Required)
  • Acute onset of respiratory symptoms within 7 days
  • Bilateral lung infiltrates on imaging
  • PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio <300 (Mild 201–300; Moderate 101–200; Severe <100)
  • PEEP >5 cm H₂O
  • Rule out cardiogenic cause of pulmonary edema
Divide PaO₂ ÷ FiO₂ to get ratio. Severe = <100.
Vent Management — Lung Protective
  • Low tidal volume (6 mL/kg IBW) — prevent barotrauma
  • High PEEP — alveolar recruitment
  • RASS goal: −3 to −5 (deeply sedated)
  • Sedation: Propofol, Ativan, Versed, Ketamine, Dexmedetomidine
  • Analgesia: Fentanyl, hydromorphone (assess with BPS or CPOT)
  • NM Blockers: Rocuronium, Cisatracurium → use Train-of-4
  • Corticosteroids
Proning
  • Improves oxygenation in severe ARDS
  • Must have RASS −4 to −5
  • Takes 4–5 staff members
  • Huge skin breakdown risk → use foam padding
  • Secure all lines/tubes before turning

Vent Alarms

  • Low pressure → circuit disconnect, airway/cuff leak
  • High pressure → coughing, biting ET, kinked tube, secretions
Tension Pneumothorax
  • In ARDS: barotrauma from high PEEP → air enters pleural space
  • S/S: absent lung sounds, tracheal deviation, hypotension, tachycardia, JVD, subcutaneous emphysema/crepitus
  • Treatment: emergent needle decompression first → then chest tube
  • Chest tube: keep drain lower than tube, NO kinks, don't clamp
  • Tidaling = normal; Bubbling = air leak
  • Output >100 mL for 2 hours → report to provider
Neurology: SCI & Stroke
SCI: 5Q · STROKE: 4Q = 9 TOTAL Dr. Jayasinghe · Cumulative
Spinal Cord Injury — Key Points
  • Higher injury = worse outcomes (C1–C4 = vent dependent, diaphragm at C1–C4)
  • Complete = total loss of motor/sensory below injury
  • Incomplete = some function preserved (Brown-Séquard: motor loss ipsilateral, pain/temp loss contralateral)
  • Acute: immobilize (log roll, C-spine), O₂ ≥95%, MAP >85, NO head ROM assessment

Complications

  • Spinal shock: sudden loss of ALL function, hours–weeks, MAP >85 (pressors/fluids)
  • Neurogenic shock: cardiovascular collapse, bradycardia (↓ BP + ↓ HR)
  • Autonomic Dysreflexia: HTN + pounding headache + cool/pale skin → HOB up, cath, identify trigger (full bladder #1)

  • Normal ICP: <15 mmHg; Goal CPP: ≥60 mmHg
  • Early signs: ↓ LOC, confusion, restlessness
  • Late signs: ↓ HR or RR, widening pulse pressure (Cushing's triad)
  • Nursing: HOB 30–45°, mannitol, hypertonic NaCl, levetiracetam, corticosteroids
  • CSF drainage (ICP monitor: yellow = normal)
  • Surgery: craniotomy (clip aneurysm), craniectomy (helmet for swelling), coil for aneurysm
CPP <50 = permanent neurological damage
Stroke Nursing Interventions
  • Aphasia: short phrases, eye contact, speech consult
  • Nutrition: dysphagia screen, thickened liquids
  • Vision: approach from intact side, call bell on intact side
  • Vitals Q15 min → Q1 hour; Neuro assessment Q1 hour
  • GU: frequent bladder scan
  • After Alteplase: NO NG tube, NO urinary catheters, NO arterial cath → bedrest

Pancreatitis — Key Points
  • Most common cause: alcohol (chronic), gallstones (acute)
  • Autodigestion: enzymes eat the pancreas itself
  • Acute: sudden mid-epigastric pain after fatty meal/alcohol, N/V
  • Labs: ↑ lipase + amylase, ↑ WBC, ↑ CRP, ↑ glucose
  • Severe: Cullen's sign (periumbilical ecchymosis), Grey Turner's sign (flank ecchymosis)
  • Treatment: NPO → advance to low-fat, no-spicy diet; no alcohol/caffeine; NG for vomiting; VTE prophylaxis
  • Shock: Blood, ABG, LR fluids
Do NOT palpate the abdomen in pancreatitis!
